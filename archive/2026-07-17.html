New Products/Approvals

HistoSonics Wins CE Mark for Edison Histotripsy System

New Products/Approvals

Core Development: On July 14, HistoSonics announced CE Mark approval for its Edison System, opening the door to commercialization across Europe and other markets that recognize the CE Mark. Edison delivers histotripsy — focused ultrasound that mechanically destroys tissue through sound-wave-driven cavitation, with no needles, heat, or radiation. The system already had FDA clearance for liver tumors in the US, dating to 2023.

Context & Impact: Histotripsy is mechanistically distinct from the thermal ablation techniques (RF, microwave, cryo) that dominate tumor-destruction today, and early data suggests it may prime the immune system against residual tumor cells — a potential edge over purely local destruction. HistoSonics has already treated over 1,000 liver-tumor patients across US, Hong Kong, and UAE sites; the CE Mark meaningfully opens up Europe's interventional radiology and hepatobiliary surgery centers as a new market.

Technical & Regulatory: Commercialization will roll out in phases, starting with high-volume liver-tumor centers. An ongoing real-world registry (BOOMBOX) is generating comparative data against thermal ablation and surgical resection; expansion into other indications (kidney, other soft tissue) is the likely next regulatory push.

Stereotaxis Completes Robocath Acquisition, Eyes Next-Gen Endovascular Robotics

Tech Innovations

Core Development: Stereotaxis finished acquiring Robocath — a Rouen, France-based robotics company for interventional cardiology and neurointervention — for up to $45M, with the deal closing July 9 and integration work continuing into this week. Robocath's technology is being folded directly into Stereotaxis's R&D roadmap to accelerate next-generation robotic tools spanning electrophysiology, interventional cardiology, and neurointerventions.

Context & Impact: Stereotaxis has long been a niche player in magnetic robotic navigation for cardiac procedures. Owning more of the robotic hardware and software stack is a bet that lets it compete more broadly across endovascular specialties rather than staying confined to electrophysiology. It's a small deal in dollar terms, but it's a consolidation signal in a robotic-surgery market that's gotten crowded this year, with Medtronic's Hugo, Stryker's Mako RPS, CMR Surgical's Versius, and Distalmotion's Dexter all jockeying for position.

Technical & Regulatory: Robocath's systems already carry CE Mark approval in Europe for neurovascular and coronary robotic interventions. Integration will focus on combining Robocath's catheter-manipulation robotics with Stereotaxis's existing navigation and mapping software; no new FDA submissions for a combined platform have been announced yet.

Clinical Advances

Envoy Medical's Fully Implanted Cochlear Implant Shows Continued Gains at 12 Months

Clinical Advances

Core Development: On July 14, Envoy Medical reported 12-month data from the first 10 patients in the pivotal trial of its Acclaim cochlear implant — the first fully implanted cochlear implant in development, with no external microphone or processor. Mean word-recognition scores rose from a 15.2% pre-implant baseline to 53.2% at 12 months, a bigger gain than the improvement reported at 6 months.

Context & Impact: Every cochlear implant on the market today requires an external sound processor worn on or behind the ear — a real source of stigma, dislodgement during exercise or sleep, and battery dependence. A device that does the whole job internally would be a genuine quality-of-life upgrade if efficacy holds up against established externals from Cochlear, MED-EL, and Advanced Bionics. That gains kept climbing between 6 and 12 months, rather than plateauing, is the most encouraging part of this readout.

Technical & Regulatory: Acclaim uses Envoy's proprietary implantable microphone, sensing eardrum vibration directly rather than relying on an external mic. This is still Stage 1 of a pivotal IDE trial with only 10 patients reported so far — a real signal, but an early and small one. A larger pivotal cohort and longer follow-up will be needed before any PMA submission.

Regulatory Updates

FTC Fines Edwards Lifesciences $10M, Genesis MedTech $2M, for Dodging Merger Review

Regulatory Updates

Core Development: The FTC and DOJ announced a $12M combined settlement on July 13 — $10M from Edwards Lifesciences and $2M from Genesis MedTech Group — resolving charges that the two companies deliberately structured Edwards's acquisition of Genesis's JC Medical unit, including a side purchase of non-voting securities, to keep the deal's voting-securities valuation under the $119.5M Hart-Scott-Rodino pre-merger notification threshold.

Context & Impact: This is the largest penalty ever levied for a Hart-Scott-Rodino filing failure, and a pointed signal from antitrust regulators that structuring deals to dodge merger-review scrutiny will get punished even in mid-sized medtech M&A, not just mega-mergers. Any device company doing bolt-on acquisitions near the HSR threshold should expect closer scrutiny of deal structure going forward.

Technical & Regulatory: Beyond the fine, Edwards must give the FTC advance notice of certain U.S. acquisitions for five years and maintain a documented antitrust compliance program — an ongoing compliance burden, not just a one-time payment.

FDA Publishes Draft MDUFA V Commitments, Congress Holds Hearing

Regulatory Updates

Core Development: The FDA published its draft Medical Device User Fee Act (MDUFA) commitments letter on July 7, laying out the performance and staffing goals the agency will meet in exchange for continued device-review user fees covering fiscal years 2028–2032. The House Energy & Commerce Subcommittee on Health held an educational hearing on the reauthorization process on July 15.

Context & Impact: MDUFA negotiations happen roughly every five years and effectively set how fast, or slow, device submissions get reviewed — this is the machinery behind every approval timeline covered in this digest. It matters because review times have already been drifting: the FDA authorized more devices in the first half of 2026 than in the same period last year, but average PMA review time rose to roughly 599 days, up from about 402 days a year earlier. The new commitments will determine whether that slide gets corrected or entrenched.

Technical & Regulatory: MDUFA is a negotiated agreement between FDA and industry (represented by AdvaMed and other trade groups) that Congress must then reauthorize by statute before the current MDUFA IV authorization lapses. Expect further public comment periods and hearings through 2027 before final reauthorization.

RapidPulse Raises $48M, With Medtronic Investing, for Stroke Thrombectomy Tech

Market Developments

Core Development: RapidPulse, a Miami-based company developing next-generation aspiration (vacuum-based) thrombectomy technology for acute ischemic stroke, closed a $48M Series B on July 16, with Medtronic among the participating investors.

Context & Impact: Strategic investment from Medtronic, already a major player in neurovascular thrombectomy, is a meaningful vote of confidence and a plausible signal of acquisition interest down the road. Stroke thrombectomy is a competitive but still-growing field as more hospitals build out stroke programs and faster time-to-treatment keeps improving outcomes; aspiration technology that clears clots faster with fewer device passes is a direct lever on complication risk, since each additional pass raises it.

Technical & Regulatory: RapidPulse has not disclosed FDA clearance status for its lead device in coverage to date; the new funding is earmarked to advance the technology toward that milestone. Worth watching for a 510(k) filing announcement in the coming months.

Struggling Robotics Developer Vicarious Surgical Heads Toward Liquidation

Market Developments

Core Development: Vicarious Surgical's board has recommended shutting down and liquidating the company, with a special shareholder meeting scheduled for July 21 to vote on dissolution.

Context & Impact: Vicarious was a high-profile, SPAC-funded surgical robotics startup built around a miniaturized, single-port robotic system, but it never reached commercialization. Its likely wind-down is a cautionary data point in a surgical robotics field that's otherwise attracting heavy investment (CMR Surgical, Distalmotion, Moon Surgical, and others) — a reminder that capital intensity and long regulatory timelines in this space claim casualties even amid overall sector enthusiasm.

Technical & Regulatory: No FDA clearance was ever obtained for its flagship system. The company's IP and remaining assets will likely be sold off or licensed following liquidation — worth watching for smaller players hunting for discounted robotics IP.
